## Method

Sources were located through DOI/Crossref-indexed search, publisher and journal
pages, author publication pages, Project Euclid/JMLR, and stable scholarly
records. Primary articles, original books, official journal records, and
author-hosted manuscripts were included when they directly fixed a theorem
family or model boundary. Surveys, course notes, tertiary summaries, and papers
whose only connection was a shared keyword were excluded from the comparison
table. Recent keyword search was used for orientation, but citation counts and
search ranking were not treated as evidence.

## Direct overlap audit for a decentralized-recovery bridge

The naive common-posterior game—each agent chooses one target and divides its
value with agents choosing the same target—is a singleton resource-selection
or congestion game. Its potential, pure-equilibrium existence, best-response
dynamics, and generic efficiency language are already classical. The
multi-round box-search model of Korman and Rodeh is closer still: it combines
multiple searchers, congestion rewards, symmetric equilibrium, success
probability, and robustness. A one-round posterior-weighted restatement would
not clear a non-overlap gate.

The smallest defensible residual object is therefore **information-conditioned
recovery**, not resource selection by itself. It must compare:

1. a named private-information discovery baseline;
2. the centralized posterior top-`L` value from DD-C-0098;
3. a precisely declared autonomous game after sharing;
4. all, best, worst, refined, or dynamically selected outcomes; and
5. the least additional coordination primitive, if any, that changes the
   implementation result.

Even that object carries high overlap risk. A future registration must include
an explicit reduction to the closest congestion/search game and identify the
one theorem statement that survives the reduction. If none survives, the
correct result is a documented non-overlap failure and a stop—not a renamed
theorem.

## Strongest risks and referee objection

The strongest risks are, in order:

1. the proposed simultaneous game is a known singleton congestion/resource-
   selection game;
2. a symmetric search equilibrium or congestion reward result is contained in
   the multiple-searcher literature;
3. “implementation” is used without the all-equilibria requirement familiar
   from implementation theory;
4. an equilibrium refinement or learning rule is imported without proving its
   discovery consequence;
5. an action license is assignment/matching in new language;
6. a coverage or reliability theorem is a planner allocation result without
   dispersed-information content; and
7. known source dependence is mistakenly presented as missing-provenance work.

The main unresolved objection a referee is likely to raise after the current
paper is: **the paper exhibits one selected decentralized improvement, but why
should that selection arise, and when do autonomous agents robustly recover the
centralized portfolio rather than a high-collision equilibrium?** That objection
is more immediate to the paper than stochastic reliability or unidentified
provenance, although the classical-overlap risk makes only a bounded bridge
appropriate.

## Audit conclusion

The literature supports testing one narrowly registered bridge before Reliable
Discovery, but not declaring a new program or broad theorem family. The bridge
must begin with a formal reduction/non-overlap audit and must be allowed to end
in a sharp overlap failure. This report does not claim novelty, priority, or
review completeness.
